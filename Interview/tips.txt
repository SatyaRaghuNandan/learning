**Resume**: 
	Keep the resume short: 	
		One page or less, remove the irrevalent materials, demonstrate respect for the interviewer's time 

	Account for all of your time: 
		Fill in the gaps with education, volunterring, additional training 
	
	Font size 10pt - 12pt, avoid novelty fonts (Arial looks great), consistent margins, Line up watermarks when printing, printing using quality paper 
	
	Develop Greater References: 
	At least three really good ones, the more varied the better;  develop references at the same time as developing  the resume; ask if a person would be willing to provide a professional reference; have a brief discussion about what you'd like them to say or not say; thank your new reference; A hand-written Thank-You note;  
	"I just wanted to send you a quick note to say 'thanks again' for agreeing to give me a professional reference. It means a lot to me that you agreed to do that, especially since I haven't been your tenant for almost a year now! I really appreciate your help, thanks again.          ----Sincerely, Fei 


**Questions for the interviewer:** 
	Don't ask  
		"what's a typical career path there?" ---- sounds like you want to jump even before getting in 
		
	Can ask 
		Who are the current team members and what do they do? What's the interaction like? How does project planning happen on the team?
		Any short-term goal of the team? Any long-term goal of the team?
		
		What brought you to this company? What has been most challenging for you at your current role?

		I notieced that you use technology X. How do you use it to handle problem Y?

		I'm very interested in X, and I'd love to learn more about it. What opportunities are there at this company to learn about it?
		I am not familiar with technology X, but it sounds like a very intersting solution. Coundl you tell me a bit more about how it woks?

		How soon will I be able to be productive? 
		What type of projects will I be able to assist on? 
		How do you measure the success of a new employer at this position?
		What's your expectation for me?
		Why is this position open? 
		Five year plan for your department? Structure/strength of your department?
		What's the favorite thing in your job?
		What the most challenging part of your job/the position I am interviewing for?
		What's the daily routine look like for this job?
		What's the vision for your department? How is this position playing in that vision? What are the goals?
		What does the daily routine look like?
		What do you look for in a candidate?
		How can I succeed at this position?
		What can I learn from this position and where will they lead me to?

**Technical questions:**
	Interviewer wants to learn about you:
	1. how do you approach a problem
	2. able to defend your answers?
	3. can communicate well?
	4. be able to pause and check your thought process
	5. demonstrate your sense, strength, weakness (honesty)

	ask how much time do I have for this question? how deep should I go?
	always clarify the questions and know how you will be evaluated
		do you like quick solution? high-quality solution? many solutions? my best solution only?

	when you stuck, talk it though creatively, why you are stuck and ask for help, "am I on the right track?"
	"I have seen a similar problem"
	"This is a very interesting problem. Sorry I can't solve it now. I am not a quick person. I will go back and send you my answer. Do you mind that?"

	Be prepared to answer hard questions:
	1. are you sure your answer is right?
	2. can you solve the question in a different way?
	3. which solution is the best?

Always ask for feedbacks (this might be the only hour in my life talking to you, can you tell me how I can improve?)
provide follow-ups
	"I really enjoyed the conersation with you. Can I have your email address?"
	In follow-up Email:
		what you taught me
		what I didn't do well but realized now
		"I'd like to hear your feedback"
	
When preparing inerview quesions, practice talking it out loud (just getting the right answer is not enough... talk the thought process out!)
